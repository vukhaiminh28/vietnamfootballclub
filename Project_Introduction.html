Week 1 Brief
Subject: Welcome to the team — your first prototype task

Hey! Welcome aboard at PixelForge. We're really excited to have you as our newest junior designer. Fair warning — it's a bit chaotic here right now. We have six weeks until our investor demo and we're one designer short, which is exactly why you're here.

Here's what I need from you this week: come up with a game concept, write the core rules, and use Claude or ChatGPT to generate some starter HTML code so we can actually click around and feel it. It doesn't need to be fancy — just something playable in a browser. We use AI here all the time to speed up the early stages, so don't be shy about it!

Quick thing about how we work at PixelForge: we always document our prompts. Any time you ask an AI something, save what you typed. We review these later to understand what worked — it's part of how we build our studio's "prompt library." So keep notes as you go.

One thing I always tell new team members: the AI is your assistant, not your brain. You still have to decide what's fun. Give it a go, make some changes yourself, and send me what you've got by Friday. The whole team is waiting to see what you come up with!

Week 2 Brief
Subject: We need visuals — can you take point on the art this week?

Hi! Marcus forwarded me your prototype from last week — honestly, really solid for a first pass. The game concept is interesting. Now I need to put a face on it. I'm the Art Director here at PixelForge, which means I'm responsible for how the entire game looks and feels — every colour choice, every character design, every background.

I know you might not be a trained illustrator, and that's totally fine — that's exactly why we use AI image tools like Flow AI and ChatGPT. What I need from you is four assets: a main hero character, one villain or obstacle, a game background scene, and a logo. These will go directly into the investor presentation, so they need to feel intentional.

The most important thing I look for as an art director isn't whether the AI made something pretty — it's whether your creative direction is clear. I can tell immediately when someone just typed "cool character" versus when someone actually thought about who this character is, what they've been through, what colours represent their personality. So write thoughtful prompts. Be specific about colours, style, mood, and posture.

One more thing: keep a "prompt diary." Write down every prompt you tried and rate each result 1–5 stars. My senior artists do this every single day — it's how we get better at directing AI tools over time. Don't skip this part.

— Sofia · s.chen@pixelforge.fake · Art Director, 3rd floor

Your role: Concept Artist at PixelForge Studios

The situation: Sofia Chen, PixelForge's Art Director, has just pulled your game concept into the visual pipeline. The investor deck goes live in 5 weeks and they need a visual identity — now. There's no time to commission a traditional illustrator.

This is exactly the kind of moment AI image tools were built for. At studios like Ubisoft and small indie teams around the world, concept artists use AI generators to produce hundreds of character sketches and environments in a single day — then pick the ones that capture the right mood and refine them. Your job is to be the creative director: you decide the look, feel, and personality of every character. The AI just draws what you describe.

Sofia's golden rule: "Anyone can click Generate. The difference between a junior and a senior artist is how precisely they can describe what they want." Think carefully before you type.

Your brief this week
JO
Jordan Okafor
Marketing Manager · PixelForge Studios
Week 3 Brief
Subject: URGENT — we need a trailer before the investor meeting!

Hey! I just got out of a call with Aisha — the investor meeting is in two weeks and I need something big from you. Sofia showed me the character art and honestly? It looks great. Now we need to show it off. I need a 30-second game trailer to open the investor presentation — something that makes people go "wait, I actually want to play that."

Here's how I'd approach it: start with the script. Think about it like a movie trailer — hook people in the first 3 seconds, show a taste of the gameplay in the middle, and land a punchy line at the end. Ask an AI to draft the script, then rewrite it in your own words so it sounds like your game, not a template. Then use Canva or Runway to animate Sofia's art into short video clips. If you can layer in a voiceover from ElevenLabs, even better — a good narrator voice makes everything feel 10x more professional.

One thing I always say to my team: the investors are going to ask "who made this?" The answer should be "I directed it, and AI helped me execute it." You are the creative lead on this trailer. Own it. Keep your script draft, save your video exports, and write a quick note about what each tool did and didn't do well. That documentation is gold for our studio portfolio.

Don't stress if it's not Hollywood quality — these investors fund indie games, not blockbusters. They want to see passion and a clear idea. You've got both. Go!

— Jordan · j.okafor@pixelforge.fake · Marketing Manager

Give them clear instructions, review every output critically, and assemble the final cut. Remember: a bad trailer can kill interest in a great game. A great trailer can sell a simple game.

Subject: The big day — time to pitch everything you've built

Hi there! I'm Aisha — I co-founded PixelForge five years ago out of a bedroom in Singapore with one laptop and too much caffeine. I don't usually write directly to junior team members, but I've been watching this project closely and I wanted you to hear from me directly.

Marcus showed me the prototype. Sofia walked me through the character designs. Jordan played me the trailer on his phone in the hallway. I have to tell you — for someone who joined this project two weeks ago and used AI tools most people are still figuring out, what you've put together is genuinely impressive. Not because it's technically perfect. Because it has a point of view.

Now comes the most important part: the pitch. On Friday, you'll present to our investor panel. These are people who've funded twenty games in the last three years — they've heard a thousand pitches. They know when someone truly believes in what they built, and when someone's just reading slides. Don't just read slides.

Tell them the story of your game. Who plays it. Why it matters. What you built. How AI helped — and where you had to take over because the AI wasn't good enough. That last part is especially important. I need you to write a one-page reflection on working with AI: what it did well, where it fell short, and what you learned. This isn't a test. This is exactly the kind of thinking I want from every person at this company. The studios that understand AI honestly are the ones still standing a decade from now.

You've earned your spot on this stage. Now go take it.

— Aisha · aisha.p@pixelforge.fake · Co-Founder & CEO, PixelForge Studios

Your role: Lead Presenter at the PixelForge Investor Demo Day

The situation: Demo Day is here. The PixelForge conference room is set up. Three investors are flying in this afternoon. Marcus, Sofia, Jordan, and Aisha are all counting on you to walk into that room and sell this game.

This is what it all comes down to. Every real game ever made went through a moment like this — a pitch. Minecraft was pitched to a Swedish games conference in 2009 before it had a single player. Among Us almost got cancelled before its fanbase discovered it years after launch. Hollow Knight raised $57,000 from backers who only saw sketches and a short video. The idea had to be communicated clearly and passionately before the game was even finished.

Now it's your turn. You have your game design, your art, your trailer — and AI is here to help you build the pitch deck and prep for the hard questions. But the most important thing in that room? Your voice, your story, your honesty about what you built and how.

Aisha asked specifically for a reflection on AI's role in this project — not because she doubts you, but because she knows the studios that understand AI honestly are the ones that will still be around in 10 years. Be thoughtful. Be real.
